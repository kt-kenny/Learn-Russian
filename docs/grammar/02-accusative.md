# Accusative Case

!!! abstract "In a nutshell"

    - Accusative marks the **direct object** — the thing directly receiving the action of the verb.
    - Masculine/neuter inanimate nouns don't change at all in the accusative. Masculine *animate* nouns (people, animals) borrow the genitive ending.
    - Feminine -а → -у, -я → -ю. Neuter never changes.
    - Accusative also answers **"куда?" (to where?)** with в/на, and shows up in set time expressions like «ка́ждый день».

Accusative is the first case to learn because it's the most common one you'll produce yourself: almost every sentence with a verb and an object needs it.

## What accusative is for

Compare:

**Кни́га на столе́.** — lit. *"Book-NOM on table."* — "The book is on the table." (кни́га is the subject — nominative)

**Я чита́ю кни́гу.** — lit. *"I read book-ACC."* — "I'm reading a book." (кни́гу is the object — accusative)

The noun changed from «кни́га» to «кни́гу» because its job in the sentence changed — from *doer* to *thing acted upon*. That's the entire point of accusative.

## Masculine and neuter: mostly unchanged

For **inanimate** masculine and all neuter nouns, the accusative form is *identical* to the nominative (dictionary) form. Nothing to learn here beyond "don't change it."

**Я чита́ю журна́л.** — lit. *"I read magazine-ACC."* — "I'm reading a magazine." (журна́л unchanged)

**Я вижу окно́.** — lit. *"I see window-ACC."* — "I see a window." (окно́ unchanged)

## Masculine animate: borrows genitive

If a masculine noun refers to a living being (person or animal), the accusative form is **not** the same as nominative — it borrows the genitive ending instead (genitive is covered fully in [Genitive](04-genitive.md), but you need this piece now).

| Nominative | Accusative (animate) | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| студе́нт → | студе́нт**а** | student |
| друг → | дру́г**а** | friend |
| кот → | кот**а́** | cat |
| Ива́н → | Ива́н**а** | Ivan |

**Я ви́жу студе́нта.** — lit. *"I see student-ACC."* — "I see a/the student." (compare: **Я ви́жу журна́л**, where the inanimate object doesn't change)

**Я люблю́ моего́ дру́га.** — lit. *"I love my-ACC friend-ACC."* — "I love my friend."

This "animate = genitive-like" rule also applies in the plural, for *all* genders — covered in the plural section below.

## Feminine: -а → -у, -я → -ю

Feminine nouns change their final vowel, whether animate or not.

| Nominative | Accusative | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| кни́га → | кни́г**у** | book |
| маши́на → | маши́н**у** | car |
| сестра́ → | сестру́ | sister |
| неде́ля → | неде́л**ю** | week |
| Мари́я → | Мари́ю | Maria |

**Я ви́жу сестру́.** — lit. *"I see sister-ACC."* — "I see my sister."

**Feminine -ь nouns don't change** in the accusative singular — they stay identical to nominative, same as masculine inanimate and neuter:

**Я люблю́ ночь.** — lit. *"I love night-ACC."* — "I love the night." (ночь unchanged)

## Neuter: never changes

Neuter nouns are identical in nominative and accusative, always — animacy doesn't affect neuter the way it affects masculine.

**Я ви́жу живо́тное.** — lit. *"I see animal-ACC."* — "I see an animal." (живо́тное, "animal," unchanged even though it's a living being — grammatical gender rules override real-world animacy here)

## Summary table

| Gender | Nominative | Accusative | Rule |
|---|---|---|---|
| Masc. inanimate | журна́л | журна́л | no change |
| Masc. animate | студе́нт | студе́нт**а** | takes genitive-like -а/-я |
| Fem. -а | кни́га | кни́г**у** | -а → -у |
| Fem. -я | неде́ля | неде́л**ю** | -я → -ю |
| Fem. -ь | ночь | ночь | no change |
| Neuter | окно́ | окно́ | no change (always) |

## Plural accusative

In the plural, the animacy rule applies across **all genders**, not just masculine:

- **Inanimate plural** → accusative = nominative plural (no change)
- **Animate plural** → accusative = genitive plural (covered in [Genitive](04-genitive.md))

**Я чита́ю кни́ги.** — lit. *"I read books-ACC."* — "I'm reading books." (inanimate plural, unchanged from nominative «кни́ги»)

**Я ви́жу студе́нтов.** — lit. *"I see students-ACC."* — "I see students." (animate plural, borrows genitive plural «студе́нтов»)

You'll drill plural genitive forms properly in Lesson 4 — for now, just recognize *why* an animate plural object looks different from its dictionary form.

## Accusative for direction: "куда?"

Accusative isn't only for direct objects — it also answers **куда?** ("to where?"), used with the prepositions **в** (into) and **на** (onto), when there's *motion toward* a place.

**Я иду́ в магази́н.** — lit. *"I go into store-ACC."* — "I'm going to the store."

**Я иду́ на рабо́ту.** — lit. *"I go onto work-ACC."* — "I'm going to work."

This is different from **где?** ("where — location"), which uses the same prepositions в/на but with the *prepositional* case instead — covered in [Prepositional](03-prepositional.md). Keep the pair in mind: в/на + accusative = motion toward; в/на + prepositional = static location.

**Compare:**

**Я в магази́не.** — lit. *"I in store-PREP."* — "I'm in the store." (location — prepositional)

**Я иду́ в магази́н.** — lit. *"I go into store-ACC."* — "I'm going to the store." (direction — accusative)

## Accusative in time expressions

Certain time expressions use accusative without needing you to think about direct objects at all — they're just fixed patterns worth memorizing:

**Я рабо́таю в сре́ду.** — lit. *"I work on Wednesday-ACC."* — "I work on Wednesday." (days of the week after «в» use accusative)

**Я чита́ю ка́ждый день.** — lit. *"I read every-ACC day-ACC."* — "I read every day." (duration/frequency expressions like «ка́ждый день,» «всю неде́лю» take accusative with no preposition at all)

## Practice

1. Put «журна́л» (magazine) into the accusative for: **Я чита́ю ___.**
2. Put «сестра́» (sister) into the accusative for: **Я ви́жу ___.**
3. Put «брат» (brother — animate!) into the accusative for: **Я люблю́ ___.**
4. Put «окно́» (window) into the accusative for: **Я мо́ю ___.** (I'm washing...)
5. Put «неде́ля» (week) into the accusative for: **Я рабо́тал всю ___.** (I worked all...)
6. Translate: "I see a cat." (кот — animate masc.)
7. Translate: "I'm going to school (шко́ла)." — use the "motion toward" construction.
8. Translate: "The book is on the table." — use the "location" construction (careful: this is NOT accusative — which case is it?)
9. Fill in: **Я рабо́таю в _____ (пя́тница — Friday).**
10. Explain in one sentence why «студе́нта» has an -а ending but «журна́л» doesn't, even though both are masculine.

??? success "Answers"

    1. **Я чита́ю журна́л.** — unchanged; masc. inanimate nouns don't change in accusative.
    2. **Я ви́жу сестру́.** — fem. -а → -у.
    3. **Я люблю́ бра́та.** — masc. animate takes the genitive-like -а ending, not the unchanged nominative.
    4. **Я мо́ю окно́.** — unchanged; neuter nouns never change in accusative.
    5. **Я рабо́тал всю неде́лю.** — fem. -я → -ю; also note «весь» agrees as «всю» (fem. acc.) — a duration expression with no preposition.
    6. **Я ви́жу кота́.** — lit. *"I see cat-ACC."* — "I see a cat." Masc. animate → genitive-like ending.
    7. **Я иду́ в шко́лу.** — lit. *"I go into school-ACC."* — "I'm going to school." «Шко́ла» is fem., so -а → -у, and «в» + accusative signals motion toward.
    8. **Кни́га на столе́.** — this is prepositional case (location, «где?»), not accusative — a trap to check you're not over-applying accusative to every noun after в/на. See [Prepositional](03-prepositional.md).
    9. **Я рабо́таю в пя́тницу.** — days of the week after «в» take accusative.
    10. «Студе́нт» refers to a living person (animate), so it borrows the genitive ending -а; «журна́л» is a thing (inanimate), so it stays identical to its nominative form. The animacy of the noun, not just its gender, decides the ending.
