# Genitive Case

!!! abstract "In a nutshell"

    - Genitive is the workhorse case: possession ("of"), the entire «у меня́ есть» ("I have") construction, negation, quantities, numbers, and a whole family of common prepositions (из, от, до, без, для, о́коло) all run through it.
    - «У меня́ нет» ("I don't have") and «не́ было / не бу́дет» always pair with genitive.
    - Numbers are strange: **2, 3, 4** take genitive *singular*; **5 and up** take genitive *plural*.
    - Plural genitive has more irregularity than any other case — including a "zero ending" pattern and fleeting vowels (окно́ → о́кон).
    - This lesson is deliberately longer than the others. Budget two study passes, not one.

Genitive earns its reputation as the hardest-working case in Russian. It's the case you'll reach for most often outside of straightforward subject/object sentences, so it's worth slowing down here.

## 1. Possession — "of"

The core meaning: genitive marks the thing something belongs to, equivalent to English "of X" or "X's."

**Э́то кни́га сестры́.** — lit. *"This book sister-GEN."* — "This is my sister's book."

**Э́то маши́на бра́та.** — lit. *"This car brother-GEN."* — "This is my brother's car."

Notice there's no word for "of" or an apostrophe-s — the genitive ending on «сестры́» and «бра́та» does that job entirely by itself.

## 2. Singular genitive endings

| Gender | Nominative | Genitive | Rule |
|---|---|---|---|
| Masc. (consonant) | студе́нт → | студе́нт**а** | add -а |
| Masc. (-ь) | слова́рь → | словар**я́** | -ь → -я |
| Masc. (-й) | музе́й → | музе́**я** | -й → -я |
| Neut. (-о) | окно́ → | окн**а́** | -о → -а |
| Neut. (-е) | мо́ре → | мо́р**я** | -е → -я |
| Fem. (-а, hard consonant) | сестра́ → | сестр**ы́** | -а → -ы |
| Fem. (-а, spelling-rule consonant) | кни́га → | кни́г**и** | -а → -и (г triggers the 7-letter rule) |
| Fem. (-я) | неде́ля → | неде́л**и** | -я → -и |
| Fem. (-ь) | дверь → | две́р**и** | -ь → -и |

**У меня́ нет словаря́.** — lit. *"At me not-exist dictionary-GEN."* — "I don't have a dictionary."

Fem. -а nouns split the same way plurals did in [Gender & Nouns](01-gender-and-nouns.md): after a hard consonant you get -ы (сестра́ → сестры́), but after one of the seven spelling-rule letters (г к х ж ш щ ч) you get -и instead (кни́га → кни́ги) — same rule, different case.

## 3. «У меня́ есть» — the possession construction

Russian has no verb "to have" in everyday use. Instead, possession is expressed as "at me there-exists X," with the possessor in genitive form (after «у») and the *thing possessed* staying in the **nominative**.

**У меня́ есть соба́ка.** — lit. *"At me exists dog-NOM."* — "I have a dog."

**У бра́та есть маши́на.** — lit. *"At brother-GEN exists car-NOM."* — "My brother has a car."

The «у» always takes its object in genitive (that's what "у + genitive" means structurally) — «меня́», «бра́та» are the genitive forms of «я» and «брат». The thing owned («соба́ка», «маши́на») is *not* genitive — it's the grammatical subject of "exists," so it stays nominative.

### Past and future

«Есть» disappears in past/future, replaced by «был/была́/бы́ло/бы́ли» (past) or «бу́дет/бу́дут» (future) — and this verb agrees with the *thing possessed*, not the possessor.

**У меня́ была́ соба́ка.** — lit. *"At me was-FEM dog-NOM."* — "I had a dog." (была́ agrees with feminine соба́ка)

**У меня́ бу́дет маши́на.** — lit. *"At me will-be car-NOM."* — "I will have a car."

**У нас бы́ли де́ньги.** — lit. *"At us were money-NOM.PL."* — "We had money." (бы́ли agrees with plural де́ньги)

### Negation: «у меня́ нет»

To say you *don't* have something, use **нет** (present), **не́ было** (past), or **не бу́дет** (future) — and critically, the thing not possessed switches to **genitive**:

**У меня́ нет соба́ки.** — lit. *"At me not-exist dog-GEN."* — "I don't have a dog." (соба́ка → соба́ки, genitive)

**У меня́ не́ было де́нег.** — lit. *"At me not was money-GEN."* — "I didn't have money."

**У меня́ не бу́дет вре́мени.** — lit. *"At me not will-be time-GEN."* — "I won't have time."

**This flip is the single most important pattern in this section:** affirmative "have" keeps the object in nominative; negative "don't have" pushes the object into genitive. «Нет», «не́ было», «не бу́дет» never change form for gender or number — they stay fixed no matter what's being negated.

## 4. Quantities and numbers

Genitive shows up any time you're saying "a lot of," "a little of," "how much/many," or a specific count of something.

### много / мало / сколько

**У меня́ мно́го книг.** — lit. *"At me much books-GEN.PL."* — "I have a lot of books."

**Ско́лько вре́мени у тебя́?** — lit. *"How-much time-GEN at you?"* — "How much time do you have?"

**У нас ма́ло де́нег.** — lit. *"At us little money-GEN."* — "We have little money."

### Numbers: 2-4 vs. 5+

This is a quirk that trips up nearly everyone. Russian numbers govern the case of the noun that follows them, and the rule changes depending on the number:

| Number | Case required | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 1 (оди́н) | nominative singular | одна́ кни́га |
| **2, 3, 4** | **genitive singular** | две кни́г**и**, три стола́, четы́ре окна́ |
| **5 and up** | **genitive plural** | пять кни́г, де́сять столо́в |

**У меня́ две кни́ги.** — lit. *"At me two book-GEN.SG."* — "I have two books." (кни́ги here is genitive *singular*, not the nominative plural — they happen to look identical for this noun, which is part of why this rule is confusing; check «стол»/«окно» below where singular and plural clearly differ)

**У меня́ три стола́.** — lit. *"At me three table-GEN.SG."* — "I have three tables." (genitive singular «стола́», not plural «столы́»)

**У меня́ пять столо́в.** — lit. *"At me five table-GEN.PL."* — "I have five tables." (genitive *plural* now that we've crossed into 5+)

Compound numbers follow the *last digit*: 21 behaves like 1, 22–24 behave like 2–4, 25–30 behave like 5+. («два́дцать оди́н стол», «два́дцать два стола́», «два́дцать пять столо́в»)

## 5. Plural genitive endings

Plural genitive is where Russian gets messy — it's the least predictable case form, and it's worth treating as semi-memorization rather than a clean rule.

| Gender/type | Nominative sg. | Genitive plural | Pattern |
|---|---|---|---|
| Masc. (consonant) | студе́нт | студе́нт**ов** | add -ов |
| Masc. (-ь, -й) | слова́рь / музе́й | словаре́**й** / музе́**ев** | add -ей / -ев |
| Neut. (-о) | окно́ | о́кон | **zero ending** + fleeting vowel |
| Neut. (-е) | мо́ре | море́**й** | add -ей |
| Fem. (-а) | кни́га | книг | **zero ending** |
| Fem. (-я) | неде́ля | неде́ль | zero ending + soft sign |
| Fem. (-ь) | дверь | двер**е́й** | add -ей |

### The "zero ending" pattern

Many feminine (-а) and neuter (-о) nouns simply **drop the ending entirely** in genitive plural — no suffix added at all:

**кни́га → книг** (no ending)

**У меня́ мно́го книг.** — lit. *"At me much books-GEN.PL."* — "I have a lot of books." (книг, bare stem, zero ending)

### Fleeting vowels

When dropping the ending would leave an awkward consonant cluster at the end of the word, Russian inserts a "fleeting" о or е to make it pronounceable — this vowel appears *only* in this form and disappears everywhere else.

**окно́ → о́кон** (not "окн" — the cluster кн is hard to end a word on, so a fleeting о is inserted)

**У до́ма пять о́кон.** — lit. *"At house-GEN five window-GEN.PL."* — "The house has five windows."

### Irregulars worth memorizing outright

| Nominative sg. | Genitive plural | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| ме́сто | мест | place(s) — zero ending, no fleeting vowel needed |
| де́ньги (plural-only) | де́нег | money — fleeting е appears |
| челове́к | люде́й* | people (*used after numbers; «люде́й» is the genitive plural of «лю́ди»/челове́к) |
| друг → друзья́ | друзе́й | friends |

**У меня́ нет де́нег.** — lit. *"At me not-exist money-GEN."* — "I don't have money." (де́ньги only exists in the plural, and its genitive plural де́нег shows the fleeting е clearly: де́ньги → де́нег)

**Здесь мно́го люде́й.** — lit. *"Here much people-GEN.PL."* — "There are a lot of people here."

## 6. Prepositions that take genitive

A cluster of very common prepositions always take their object in genitive. Worth memorizing as a set:

| Preposition | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **из** | out of, from (origin) | Я из Росси́и. — "I'm from Russia." |
| **от** | away from, from (a person/source) | Письмо́ от дру́га. — "A letter from a friend." |
| **до** | until, up to | До за́втра! — "Until tomorrow!" |
| **без** | without | Ко́фе без са́хара. — "Coffee without sugar." |
| **для** | for (the benefit of) | Э́то пода́рок для тебя́. — "This is a gift for you." |
| **о́коло** | near, approximately | О́коло до́ма. — "Near the house." |

**Я из Москвы́.** — lit. *"I from Moscow-GEN."* — "I'm from Moscow."

**Э́то кни́га для тебя́.** — lit. *"This book for you-GEN."* — "This book is for you."

## Practice

1. Put «сестра́» into genitive for possession: **Э́то маши́на ___.** ("This is [my] sister's car.")
2. Fill in the affirmative "have": **У меня́ ___ (есть/был) кни́га.** in the *past tense*.
3. Fill in the negative: **У меня́ ___ кни́ги.** (I don't have a book — present tense.)
4. Put «пять» + «студе́нт» together correctly.
5. Put «два» + «окно́» together correctly.
6. Give the genitive plural of «кни́га».
7. Give the genitive plural of «окно́», and name the phenomenon that produces its form.
8. Translate: "I don't have money." (де́ньги)
9. Fill in the correct preposition + case: **Ко́фе ___ са́хара.** (coffee without sugar)
10. Translate: "There are a lot of people here." (use «мно́го»)
11. Why does «У меня́ есть кни́га» use nominative «кни́га», but «У меня́ нет кни́ги» switches to genitive?
12. Give the genitive plural of «друг» (irregular — recall the plural stem from [Gender & Nouns](01-gender-and-nouns.md)).

??? success "Answers"

    1. **Э́то маши́на сестры́.** — genitive marks possession; сестра́ → сестры́. Fem. -а nouns take genitive -ы after hard consonants (р is hard) and -и after the 7 spelling-rule letters (see [Gender & Nouns](01-gender-and-nouns.md)) — «кни́га» → «кни́ги» uses -и because г is one of those letters, while «сестра́» → «сестры́» uses plain -ы because р is not.
    2. **У меня́ была́ кни́га.** — past tense "have" uses был/была́/бы́ло agreeing with the possessed noun; кни́га is feminine, so была́.
    3. **У меня́ нет кни́ги.** — negation forces genitive; кни́га → кни́ги.
    4. **пять студе́нтов** — 5+ triggers genitive plural; студе́нт → студе́нтов.
    5. **два окна́** — 2–4 triggers genitive *singular*; окно́ → окна́ (genitive sg., not the nominative plural о́кна — note the stress difference: genitive sg. окна́ vs. nominative pl. о́кна).
    6. **книг** — zero ending, feminine -а nouns commonly drop the ending entirely in genitive plural.
    7. **о́кон** — zero ending plus a fleeting о inserted to avoid the unpronounceable cluster "окн" at the end of a word.
    8. **У меня́ нет де́нег.** — lit. *"At me not-exist money-GEN."* «Де́ньги» is plural-only; its genitive plural де́нег shows a fleeting е.
    9. **Ко́фе без са́хара.** — «без» always takes genitive; са́хар → са́хара.
    10. **Здесь мно́го люде́й.** — lit. *"Here much people-GEN.PL."* «Мно́го» always takes a genitive object, and «люде́й» is the genitive plural used for people/челове́к.
    11. Affirmative «есть» constructions treat the possessed thing as the grammatical subject of "exists," so it stays nominative; negation flips the logic to "of X, none exists," which requires genitive on the thing being negated — this is a fixed pattern, not optional.
    12. **друзе́й** — irregular; the plural stem друзья́ (see [Gender & Nouns](01-gender-and-nouns.md)) takes -ей in the genitive plural: друзе́й.
