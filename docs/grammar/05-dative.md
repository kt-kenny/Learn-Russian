# Dative Case

!!! abstract "In a nutshell"

    - Dative marks the **indirect object** — the "to/for whom" of a sentence.
    - «Мне нра́вится» ("I like," lit. "to-me is-pleasing") is dative-based and works differently from «люби́ть» ("to love/like").
    - Age is expressed with dative: «мне 25 лет» — lit. "to-me 25 years."
    - Нужно, можно, нельзя ("need to / may / must not") all take a dative "experiencer" instead of a nominative subject.
    - Prepositions к ("toward") and по ("along/according to") take dative; so do a cluster of impersonal weather/feeling expressions like «мне хо́лодно».

## What dative is for

Dative marks the person or thing something is given *to*, done *for*, or experienced *by* — the receiving end of an action, as opposed to accusative's "thing directly acted on."

**Я даю́ кни́гу дру́гу.** — lit. *"I give book-ACC friend-DAT."* — "I'm giving the book to a friend."

Here «кни́гу» (accusative) is what's given; «дру́гу» (dative) is who it's given *to*. Two different objects, two different cases, one sentence.

| Masc. (-ь) | слова́рь → | словар**ю́** | -ь → -ю |
| Neut. (-о) | окно́ → | окн**у́** | -о → -у |
| Neut. (-е) | мо́ре → | мо́р**ю** | -е → -ю |
| Fem. (-а) | кни́га → | кни́г**е** | -а → -е |
| Fem. (-я) | неде́ля → | неде́л**е** | -я → -е |
| Fem. (-ь) | дверь → | две́р**и** | -ь → -и |

**Я звоню́ ма́ме.** — lit. *"I call mom-DAT."* — "I'm calling mom." (ма́ма → ма́ме)

**Он пи́шет письмо́ учи́телю.** — lit. *"He writes letter-ACC teacher-DAT."* — "He's writing a letter to the teacher."

## Dative pronouns

| Nominative | Dative | Example |
|---|---|---|
| я | мне | Дай мне кни́гу. — "Give me the book." |
| ты | тебе́ | Я звоню́ тебе́. — "I'm calling you." |
| он / оно́ | ему́ | Я даю́ ему́ ру́чку. — "I'm giving him a pen." |
| она́ | ей | Я звоню́ ей. — "I'm calling her." |
| мы | нам | Он пи́шет нам. — "He's writing to us." |
| вы | вам | Я говорю́ вам пра́вду. — "I'm telling you the truth." |
| они́ | им | Мы пи́шем им. — "We're writing to them." |

These pronoun forms appear constantly — much more often than you'll consciously produce noun dative endings — so they're worth over-learning early.

## «Мне нра́вится» vs. «люби́ть»

Russian has two ways to say "I like," and they work on opposite grammatical logic.

**«Нра́вится»** literally means "is pleasing" — the *thing liked* is the grammatical subject (nominative), and the *person who likes it* is in dative. The verb agrees with the thing liked, not the person.

**Мне нра́вится ко́фе.** — lit. *"To-me is-pleasing coffee-NOM."* — "I like coffee." (ко́фе is the subject, singular → нра́вится)

**Мне нра́вятся кни́ги.** — lit. *"To-me are-pleasing books-NOM."* — "I like books." (кни́ги is plural → нра́вятся, the verb changes to match)

**Ему́ нра́вится э́тот фильм.** — lit. *"To-him is-pleasing this film-NOM."* — "He likes this film."

**«Люби́ть»** ("to love/like") works the normal way — the liker is the nominative subject, and the thing liked is a direct object in accusative:

**Я люблю́ ко́фе.** — lit. *"I love coffee-ACC."* — "I love coffee." (я is the subject; ко́фе happens to be unchanged since it's an indeclinable loanword, but grammatically it's the accusative object)

**Use «нра́вится» for "I like [this specific thing/experience]," and «люби́ть» for stronger, more general "I love [X]."** In casual speech both are common for everyday preferences, but the grammar underneath is genuinely different — don't try to conjugate «нра́вится» like a normal verb with «я» as its subject; the person always sits in dative.

## Age: «мне 25 лет»

Age is expressed with the person in dative plus a number and «год/го́да/лет» (year/years) — there's no verb "to be" in the present tense here at all.

**Мне 25 лет.** — lit. *"To-me 25 years."* — "I am 25 years old."

**Ей два́дцать оди́н год.** — lit. *"To-her 21 year."* — "She is 21 years old." (год — singular, because оди́н triggers nominative singular, same logic as [Genitive §4](04-genitive.md))

**Ему́ три го́да.** — lit. *"To-him 3 years."* — "He is 3 years old." (го́да — genitive singular, 2-4 pattern)

**Нам два́дцать пять лет.** — lit. *"To-us 25 years."* — "We are 25 years old." (лет — genitive plural, 5+ pattern)

The год/го́да/лет choice follows exactly the number-agreement rule from [Genitive](04-genitive.md#4-quantities-and-numbers): 1 → nominative singular (год), 2-4 → genitive singular (го́да), 5+ (and 11-20) → genitive plural (лет).

## Нужно / можно / нельзя + dative

These three impersonal words don't have a normal subject — the person affected goes in dative instead, and the word itself never changes form.

**Мне ну́жно рабо́тать.** — lit. *"To-me [it-is] necessary to-work."* — "I need to work."

**Тебе́ мо́жно войти́.** — lit. *"To-you [it-is] permitted to-enter."* — "You may come in."

**Ему́ нельзя́ кури́ть.** — lit. *"To-him [it-is] forbidden to-smoke."* — "He must not smoke."

Compare to English, where "I need," "you may," "he must not" all put the person as a normal subject with a conjugated verb — Russian instead treats these as fixed impersonal expressions that only change who's affected via the dative pronoun/noun in front.

## Impersonal constructions: мне хо́лодно, мне ка́жется

A whole family of "state" expressions work the same way: dative person + an unchanging short-form adjective or adverb, no subject at all.

**Мне хо́лодно.** — lit. *"To-me [it-is] cold."* — "I'm cold."

**Мне жа́рко.** — lit. *"To-me [it-is] hot."* — "I'm hot."

**Мне ка́жется, что э́то пра́вда.** — lit. *"To-me it-seems that this [is] truth."* — "It seems to me that it's true."

**Ему́ ску́чно.** — lit. *"To-him [it-is] boring."* — "He's bored."

Notice English "I'm cold" makes *you* the subject; Russian «мне хо́лодно» makes *you* the dative experiencer of an impersonal state — there's no real subject in the sentence at all. This pattern is extremely common in everyday speech, so it's worth drilling as fixed phrases.

## Prepositions к and по

**к** ("toward/to," motion approaching but not necessarily entering) always takes dative:

**Я иду́ к врачу́.** — lit. *"I go toward doctor-DAT."* — "I'm going to the doctor('s)."

Compare this to accusative's «в/на + accusative» for motion *into* a place ([Accusative §Direction](02-accusative.md#accusative-for-direction)) — «к» is used when you're heading toward a person or a general direction, not entering an enclosed space.

**по** ("along," "according to," or with days meaning "on [recurring day]") also takes dative:

**Я гуля́ю по па́рку.** — lit. *"I walk along park-DAT."* — "I'm walking through the park."

**По понеде́льникам я рабо́таю.** — lit. *"Along Mondays-DAT I work."* — "On Mondays I work." (recurring day — a different construction from accusative's one-time «в сре́ду», see [Accusative §Time](02-accusative.md#accusative-in-time-expressions))

## Practice

1. Put «друг» into dative: **Я даю́ кни́гу ___.**
2. Put «я» into dative: **___ ну́жно рабо́тать.**
3. Translate using «нра́вится»: "I like this book (fem. sg.)."
4. Translate using «люби́ть»: "I love my mother."
5. Say your age using the pattern «мне ... лет», for age 22 (два́дцать два).
6. Translate: "I'm cold."
7. Fill in: **Мне ___, что он прав.** ("It seems to me that he's right.")
8. Put «врач» (doctor) after «к»: **Я иду́ ___.**
9. Translate: "You (informal) may enter." — use «мо́жно».
10. Explain the grammatical difference between «Мне нра́вится ко́фе» and «Я люблю́ ко́фе».

??? success "Answers"

    1. **Я даю́ кни́гу дру́гу.** — masc. dative adds -у.
    2. **Мне ну́жно рабо́тать.** — «ну́жно» takes a dative experiencer, я → мне.
    3. **Мне нра́вится э́та кни́га.** — the book is the grammatical subject (nominative, singular → нра́вится); "I" goes to dative «мне».
    4. **Я люблю́ свою́ мать.** (or «мою́ ма́му») — «люби́ть» works like a normal verb: I = nominative subject, mother = accusative object.
    5. **Мне два́дцать два го́да.** — 22 ends in 2, which triggers genitive singular «го́да», following the same 2-4 rule as [Genitive](04-genitive.md).
    6. **Мне хо́лодно.** — lit. *"To-me [it-is] cold."* Impersonal construction, dative experiencer, no real subject.
    7. **Мне ка́жется, что он прав.** — fixed impersonal phrase «мне ка́жется».
    8. **Я иду́ к врачу́.** — «к» always takes dative; врач → врачу́.
    9. **Тебе́ мо́жно войти́.** — «мо́жно» takes dative ты → тебе́, the verb «войти́» stays in the infinitive.
    10. In «Мне нра́вится ко́фе», «ко́фе» is the nominative subject (the thing doing the "pleasing") and "I" is the dative experiencer; in «Я люблю́ ко́фе», "I" is the nominative subject doing the loving, and «ко́фе» is the accusative object being loved. Same rough meaning, reversed grammatical roles.
