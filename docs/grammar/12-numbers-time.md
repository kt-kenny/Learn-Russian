# Numbers & Time

!!! abstract "In a nutshell"

    - Оди́н agrees in gender (оди́н/одна́/одно́/одни́); два/две splits by gender too (два for masc/neuter, две for feminine) — every other number stays fixed.
    - Numbers control the case of the noun that follows: 1 → nominative singular, 2–4 → genitive singular, 5+ (and 0) → genitive plural — and this depends on the **last digit** of the number, even in compounds like 21 or 102.
    - Days use **в** + accusative; months use **в** + prepositional; dates use a neuter ordinal in the nominative or genitive depending on context.
    - Age uses the same dative construction ("мне... лет") you saw in the [Dative Case lesson](05-dative.md) — this lesson just adds the numbers to plug into it.

Compound numbers just stack the pieces together, same as English: **два́дцать оди́н** (21, literally "twenty-one"), **три́дцать пять** (35), **сто два́дцать три** (123).

## Оди́н and два/две agree with gender

Most numbers are fixed words that never change no matter what they're counting. **Оди́н** ("one") is the big exception — it fully agrees with the gender of its noun, just like an adjective:

| Masculine | Feminine | Neuter | Plural |
|---|---|---|---|
| оди́н | одна́ | одно́ | одни́ |

**У меня́ оди́н брат.** — lit. *"At me one-masc brother."* — "I have one brother."

**У меня́ одна́ сестра́.** — lit. *"At me one-fem sister."* — "I have one sister."

**Два** ("two") splits only by gender, not the full four-way split: **два** for masculine and neuter, **две** for feminine.

**У меня́ два бра́та.** — lit. *"At me two brother-GEN.SG."* — "I have two brothers."

**У меня́ две сестры́.** — lit. *"At me two sister-GEN.SG."* — "I have two sisters."

Три (3) and четы́ре (4) don't change for gender at all — only оди́н and два/две do.

## Numbers control the case of the noun

This is the part that surprises English speakers most: **the number itself decides what case (and sometimes number) the following noun takes.** The rule has three bands:

1. **1** (and any compound number ending in 1, like 21, 31, 101 — except 11) + **nominative singular** noun
2. **2, 3, 4** (and compounds ending in 2, 3, 4, like 22, 23, 24 — except 12, 13, 14) + **genitive singular** noun
3. **5–20, 0, and any number ending in 5–9 or 0** + **genitive plural** noun

| Number | Noun form | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | nominative singular | **одна́ кни́га** — one book |
| 2–4 | genitive singular | **две кни́ги** — two books |
| 5+ | genitive plural | **пять книг** — five books |
| 21 | nominative singular | **два́дцать одна́ кни́га** — 21 books |
| 22 | genitive singular | **два́дцать две кни́ги** — 22 books |
| 25 | genitive plural | **два́дцать пять книг** — 25 books |

**У меня́ два́дцать одна́ кни́га.** — lit. *"At me twenty one-fem book-NOM.SG."* — "I have 21 books."

**У меня́ два́дцать две кни́ги.** — lit. *"At me twenty two book-GEN.SG."* — "I have 22 books."

**У меня́ два́дцать пять книг.** — lit. *"At me twenty five book-GEN.PL."* — "I have 25 books."

The key thing to notice: it's the **last digit** that decides the pattern, no matter how big the whole number is. 101 behaves like 1 (101 кни́га); 102 behaves like 2 (102 кни́ги); 111 behaves like the 5+ group — **11–14 are always genitive plural**, even though 11 "ends in 1" — this is the one place the last-digit rule has a built-in exception, so learn 11–14 as a fixed genitive-plural zone.

This exact system — numbers governing genitive singular or genitive plural — is the same genitive mechanism from [Lesson 4: Genitive](04-genitive.md)'s quantities section; this lesson is just applying it with real numbers attached.

## Telling time (simplified)

At A2, you need the basics: "what time is it" and "at what time." Full quarter-past/quarter-to phrasing is more advanced and can wait.

**Ско́лько сейча́с вре́мени?** — lit. *"How-much now time-GEN?"* — "What time is it now?"

**Сейча́с три часа́.** — lit. *"Now three hour-GEN.SG."* — "It's three o'clock."

**Сейча́с пять часо́в.** — lit. *"Now five hour-GEN.PL."* — "It's five o'clock." (notice часа́ vs часо́в follows the same 2–4/5+ noun-case rule you just learned)

To say "at" a certain time, use **в** + accusative (which looks identical to nominative for hour numbers at this simple level):

**В два часа́ я обе́даю.** — lit. *"At two hour-GEN.SG I dine."* — "I have lunch at two o'clock."

**Я встаю́ в семь часо́в.** — lit. *"I get-up at seven hour-GEN.PL."* — "I get up at seven o'clock."

## Days of the week

Days use **в** + accusative case.

| Russian | English |
|---|---|
| понеде́льник | Monday |
| вто́рник | Tuesday |
| среда́ | Wednesday |
| четве́рг | Thursday |
| пя́тница | Friday |
| суббо́та | Saturday |
| воскресе́нье | Sunday |

**Я рабо́таю в понеде́льник.** — lit. *"I work on Monday-ACC."* — "I work on Monday."

**Мы встреча́емся во вто́рник.** — lit. *"We meet on Tuesday-ACC."* — "We're meeting on Tuesday." (в becomes во before the consonant cluster вт-, purely for pronunciation ease)

## Dates and years

For "what's today's date," Russian uses a **neuter ordinal in the nominative** (dates agree with the implied word число́, "date/number," which is neuter):

**Како́е сего́дня число́?** — lit. *"Which today number-NOM?"* — "What's today's date?"

**Сего́дня пе́рвое января́.** — lit. *"Today first-neut January-GEN."* — "Today is January 1st." (the ordinal is neuter nominative; the month name switches to genitive, "of January")

For "in what year," use **в како́м году́** + the ordinal in the **prepositional** case:

**В како́м году́ ты роди́лся?** — lit. *"In which-PREP year-PREP you were-born?"* — "What year were you born in?"

**Я роди́лся в две ты́сячи пя́том году́.** — lit. *"I was-born in two thousand fifth-PREP year-PREP."* — "I was born in 2005."

This is one of the trickiest constructions in the whole numbers system — for now, focus on recognizing the pattern (year expressed as an ordinal, in prepositional case, after в) rather than producing complex years fluently.

## Age: the dative construction, recapped

You met this construction's grammar in the [Dative Case lesson](05-dative.md) — age in Russian is expressed as "to me [there] are ___ years," using the person in dative and a number + word for "year(s)."

**Мне два́дцать лет.** — lit. *"To-me twenty year-GEN.PL."* — "I'm twenty (years old)."

**Ему́ три́дцать лет.** — lit. *"To-him thirty year-GEN.PL."* — "He's thirty."

**Ей два́дцать оди́н год.** — lit. *"To-her twenty one year-NOM.SG."* — "She's twenty-one."

Notice год/го́да/лет follows the exact same 1 / 2–4 / 5+ pattern as every other counted noun: **оди́н год** (nominative singular), **два го́да** (genitive singular), **пять лет** (genitive plural — irregular-looking, but лет is simply the genitive plural of год that's used here by convention).

## Practice

1. Say "one sister" with correct agreement (одна́/оди́н/одно́ + сестра́).
2. Say "two brothers" with correct agreement (два/две + брат in the right form).
3. What case/number does the noun take after 5, 6, 7...? Give an example with кни́га.
4. Fill in the blank: У меня́ два́дцать три ___ (кни́га — correct form).
5. Fill in the blank: У меня́ два́дцать одна́ ___ (кни́га — correct form).

7. Translate: "I work on Fridays." (use в + пя́тница)

??? success "Answers"

    1. **одна́ сестра́** — feminine agreement of оди́н.
    2. **два бра́та** — masculine noun, so два (not две); брат takes genitive singular.
    3. Genitive plural — **пять книг**.
    4. У меня́ два́дцать три **кни́ги**. — ends in 3, so genitive singular.
    5. У меня́ два́дцать одна́ **кни́га**. — ends in 1, so nominative singular.
    6. **Сейча́с четы́ре часа́.** — часа́ (genitive singular) after 2–4.
    7. **Я рабо́таю в пя́тницу.** — days take в + accusative.
    8. **Мой день рожде́ния в сентябре́.** — months take в + prepositional.
    9. **тре́тий** — irregular-looking ordinal for 3rd.
    10. **Како́е сего́дня число́?** — fixed idiom asking for the date.
    11. **Мне три́дцать лет.** — dative subject + genitive plural лет.
    12. 11–14 are a fixed exception zone — they always take genitive plural regardless of their final digit, unlike 21–24 (and other compounds), which follow the last-digit rule normally.
