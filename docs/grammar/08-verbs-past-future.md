# Verbs — Past & Future Tense

!!! abstract "In a nutshell"

    - Past tense agrees with **gender and number** of the subject, not person — one set of four endings covers every "I/you/he/she/we/they."
    - A handful of common verbs have irregular past stems: идти́ → шёл/шла́/шло́/шли́, мочь → мог/могла́/могло́/могли́.
    - Compound future (**бу́ду** + imperfective infinitive) works like English "will be doing" — full paradigm below.
    - Perfective verbs have their *own* future form that looks like a present-tense conjugation but means "will finish" — the full explanation is [Lesson 9: Aspect](09-aspect.md).

## Past tense: built from gender and number, not person

This is the single biggest structural difference from present tense: Russian past-tense verbs don't care whether the subject is "I," "you," or "she" — they only care whether the subject is masculine, feminine, neuter, or plural. Strip the **-ть** off the infinitive and add one of four endings:

| Subject gender/number | Ending | чита́ть → | говори́ть → |
|---|---|---|---|
| Masculine singular (я/ты/он) | **-л** | чита́**л** | говори́**л** |
| Feminine singular (я/ты/она́) | **-ла** | чита́**ла** | говори́**ла** |
| Neuter singular (оно́) | **-ло** | чита́**ло** | говори́**ло** |
| Plural (мы/вы/они́) | **-ли** | чита́**ли** | говори́**ли** |

**Я чита́л кни́гу.** — lit. *"I read-masc book-ACC."* — "I read a book." (said by a male speaker)

**Я чита́ла кни́гу.** — lit. *"I read-fem book-ACC."* — "I read a book." (said by a female speaker)

**Они́ говори́ли по-ру́сски.** — lit. *"They spoke-plural in-Russian-way."* — "They spoke Russian."

Notice that "я" and "ты" each need *you* to supply the gender based on who's talking — the verb form itself doesn't distinguish 1st from 2nd person at all. This is genuinely simpler than present tense in one sense (only four forms to learn instead of six) — you just have to retrain the reflex of "which person" into "which gender."

## Irregular past stems

A few very common verbs don't just glue -л onto the infinitive stem — the masculine form in particular often drops the -л entirely when the stem ends in a consonant.

### идти́ (to go, on foot) → шёл/шла́/шло́/шли́

| Gender/number | Form |
|---|---|
| Masculine | шёл |
| Feminine | шла́ |
| Neuter | шло́ |
| Plural | шли́ |

**Он шёл домо́й.** — lit. *"He went(masc) homeward."* — "He was walking home."

Notice the stress moves onto the ending in feminine/neuter/plural (шла́, шло́, шли́) but sits on the stem in masculine (шёл) — this shifting stress pattern is common among irregular past-tense verbs, so don't assume stress stays put.

### мочь (to be able to) → мог/могла́/могло́/могли́

| Gender/number | Form |
|---|---|
| Masculine | мог |
| Feminine | могла́ |
| Neuter | могло́ |
| Plural | могли́ |

**Я не мог позвони́ть тебе́.** — lit. *"I not could to-call you."* — "I couldn't call you." (male speaker)

Notice **мог**, not "могл" — verb stems ending in a consonant (г here) simply skip the -л in the masculine form, because "могл" is impossible to pronounce cleanly in Russian. This masculine-drops-л pattern shows up in a small set of consonant-stem verbs; treat each one as vocabulary to memorize rather than trying to derive it from a universal rule.

### есть (to eat) → ел/е́ла/е́ло/е́ли

| Gender/number | Form |
|---|---|
| Masculine | ел |
| Feminine | е́ла |
| Neuter | е́ло |
| Plural | е́ли |

**Мы е́ли пи́ццу вчера́.** — lit. *"We ate pizza-ACC yesterday."* — "We ate pizza yesterday."

### быть (to be) — был/была́/бы́ло/бы́ли

**быть** is the single most-used irregular past verb, because it forms half of common negative constructions.

| Gender/number | Form |
|---|---|
| Masculine | был |
| Feminine | была́ |
| Neuter | бы́ло |
| Plural | бы́ли |

**Вчера́ я был до́ма.** — lit. *"Yesterday I was(masc) at-home."* — "Yesterday I was home." (male speaker)

**Она́ была́ на рабо́те.** — lit. *"She was(fem) at work-PREP."* — "She was at work."

Notice the stress: feminine была́ moves the stress to the ending, but masculine/neuter/plural keep it on the stem (был, бы́ло, бы́ли). This is a very common and very tested stress pattern — drill it separately from the meaning.

**Negative past of быть is special.** The negative particle не combines with был/была́/бы́ло/бы́ли, but in the neuter/impersonal negative form, the stress *always* jumps to не, and the form is **не́ было** regardless of what's being negated:

**У меня́ не́ было вре́мени.** — lit. *"At me not-was time-GEN."* — "I didn't have time."

**Его́ не́ было до́ма.** — lit. *"Him not-was at-home."* — "He wasn't home."

Compare this to the plain negative of the masculine/feminine/plural forms, which behave normally (не был, не была́, не́ были) — но **не́ было** is the fixed, frozen "there wasn't/there wasn't any" form and is worth memorizing as its own chunk.

## Future tense: two systems

Russian doesn't have one future tense — it has **two**, and which one you use depends on aspect (imperfective vs. perfective). This lesson introduces the shape of both; [Lesson 9: Aspect](09-aspect.md) is where you'll learn to actually *choose* between them with confidence.

### Compound future — for imperfective verbs

Built from a conjugated form of **быть** (to be) plus an imperfective infinitive. This describes an ongoing action, a process, or something you'll be doing/doing repeatedly in the future — think English "I will be reading" or plain "I will read [as an activity]."

| | быть | + infinitive |
|---|---|---|
| я | бу́ду | бу́ду чита́ть |
| ты | бу́дешь | бу́дешь чита́ть |
| он/она́/оно́ | бу́дет | бу́дет чита́ть |
| мы | бу́дем | бу́дем чита́ть |
| вы | бу́дете | бу́дете чита́ть |
| они́ | бу́дут | бу́дут чита́ть |

**Я бу́ду чита́ть весь ве́чер.** — lit. *"I will-be to-read whole evening."* — "I'll be reading all evening."

**Мы бу́дем жить в Москве́.** — lit. *"We will-be to-live in Moscow-PREP."* — "We're going to live in Moscow."

The infinitive after **бу́ду** etc. must be imperfective — this pairing (a conjugated бу́ду + imperfective infinitive) is the *only* correct way to build this kind of future. You cannot say "бу́ду прочита́ть" — that mixes a compound-future construction with a perfective infinitive, and it's simply ungrammatical.

### Perfective future — for perfective verbs

Perfective verbs (like прочита́ть, "to read through / finish reading") don't use бу́ду at all. Instead, you conjugate the perfective verb *directly*, using endings that look exactly like present-tense endings — but because the verb is perfective, the meaning is automatically future ("will finish," "will complete"), never "am doing right now."

**Я прочита́ю кни́гу за́втра.** — lit. *"I through-read(future) book-ACC tomorrow."* — "I'll finish reading the book tomorrow."

**Он напи́шет письмо́.** — lit. *"He through-write(future) letter-ACC."* — "He'll write the letter (and finish it)."

This might feel confusing right now — the same verb shape you'd expect to mean "I read [now]" instead means "I will finish reading," simply because the verb is perfective and perfective verbs have no present tense at all. That's exactly the topic of the next lesson: **[Lesson 9: Aspect](09-aspect.md)** goes deep on why Russian splits verbs into imperfective/perfective pairs, and how to pick the right one for compound vs. perfective future, and for past tense too.

## Practice

1. Conjugate **чита́ть** in the past tense for a female subject "она́."
2. Conjugate **говори́ть** in the past tense for the plural subject "мы."
3. Give the past tense of **быть** for a neuter subject ("оно́ ... до́ма").
4. Translate: "I didn't have money." (use у меня́ + не́ было + де́нег)
5. Give all four past-tense forms of **идти́**.
6. Give all four past-tense forms of **мочь**. Why is there no "л" in the masculine form?
7. Conjugate **бу́ду говори́ть** for "ты" and "они́."
8. Translate using compound future: "We will be working tomorrow." (рабо́тать)
9. True or false: "бу́ду прочита́ть" is a correct way to say "I will read (and finish) it." Explain.
10. Translate: "She ate breakfast." (use есть, за́втрак, feminine subject)
11. Fill in the blank: Вчера́ он ___ (быть) на рабо́те.
12. Explain in one sentence why "я чита́л" and "я чита́ла" mean the same thing in English but are different words in Russian.

??? success "Answers"

    1. **чита́ла** — feminine singular past ending -ла.
    2. **говори́ли** — plural past ending -ли, same for any plural subject regardless of person.
    3. **бы́ло** — neuter singular past of быть, stress on the stem.
    4. **У меня́ не́ было де́нег.** — fixed negative-past-of-быть construction, always "не́ было" + genitive.
    5. **шёл, шла́, шло́, шли́** — irregular стем, stress shifts to the ending except in masculine.
    6. **мог, могла́, могло́, могли́** — no masculine "л" because the consonant-ending stem (мог-) can't take -л smoothly; this is a memorized irregularity.
    7. ты **бу́дешь говори́ть**, они́ **бу́дут говори́ть** — бу́ду conjugated for person, plus the plain imperfective infinitive.
    8. **Мы бу́дем рабо́тать за́втра.** — compound future, imperfective рабо́тать describes the activity itself.
    9. **False.** Perfective verbs never combine with бу́ду — a perfective verb is conjugated directly (я прочита́ю) to express future meaning.
    10. **Она́ е́ла за́втрак.** — irregular есть, feminine past form.
    11. Вчера́ он **был** на рабо́те. — masculine singular past of быть.
    12. Past tense agrees with the *gender of the speaker/subject*, not with "I" as a fixed grammatical person — so a male and female speaker both saying "I read" use different verb forms.
