# Verbal Aspect

!!! abstract "In a nutshell"

    - Every Russian verb (with rare exceptions) belongs to one of two **aspects**: imperfective (process, repetition, fact-without-result) or perfective (a single completed action, viewed as one whole event).
    - Aspect is usually shown by a **pair** of verbs — often related by a prefix (де́лать → сде́лать) or a suffix change (покупа́ть → купи́ть) — that you learn together, not as separate vocabulary.
    - Aspect changes the *shape* of past and future tense, and even changes the *feel* of a negated sentence.
    - This is genuinely the hardest single grammar topic in Russian for English speakers — budget real time for it, and expect to keep refining your instinct for months after this lesson.

This is the lesson the [roadmap](../roadmap.md) gives two full weeks to, and for good reason. English marks completion with helper words and context ("I read a book" vs. "I finished reading the book" vs. "I was reading a book"). Russian marks it by using a **different verb** — and choosing the right one is not optional; it changes what your sentence means.

## What aspect actually is

Think of aspect as answering the question: **how is this action being viewed — as an ongoing process, or as a single completed whole?**

- **Imperfective** (несоверше́нный вид) verbs describe:
    - an action in progress ("I was reading")
    - a repeated or habitual action ("I read every day")
    - the plain *fact* that an action happened or happens, without focus on whether it was finished ("I read [that book] before — I've done that activity")
- **Perfective** (соверше́нный вид) verbs describe:
    - a single completed action, viewed as one whole event, usually with a result ("I read the whole book — it's done")
    - a single action about to start or about to finish at one specific moment

Neither aspect is "more correct" — they're two different lenses on the same underlying action, and Russian forces you to pick one every time you use a verb.

## Aspectual pairs

Almost every Russian verb comes in an imperfective/perfective **pair** — two different words that share a core meaning but differ in aspect. You don't learn "read" as one word; you learn **чита́ть / прочита́ть** as a pair, from day one.

| Imperfective | Perfective | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| чита́ть | прочита́ть | to read |
| писа́ть | написа́ть | to write |
| де́лать | сде́лать | to do/make |
| покупа́ть | купи́ть | to buy |
| говори́ть | сказа́ть | to speak/say |
| брать | взять | to take |
| дава́ть | дать | to give |
| смотре́ть | посмотре́ть | to watch/look |
| есть | съесть | to eat |
| пить | вы́пить | to drink |

## How pairs are formed

There's no single rule, but three patterns cover almost everything you'll meet at A2.

### 1. Prefix added to the imperfective

The most common pattern: a small prefix glued onto the front of the imperfective verb turns it perfective. The prefix often (not always) carries a literal spatial or completive meaning.

**де́лать → сде́лать** ("do" → "do-through," i.e., get it done)

**писа́ть → написа́ть** ("write" → "onto-write," i.e., write it out fully)

**Она́ де́лает дома́шнее зада́ние.** — lit. *"She does homework-ACC."* — "She's doing her homework (right now, in progress)."

**Она́ сде́лала дома́шнее зада́ние.** — lit. *"She through-did homework-ACC."* — "She finished her homework."

### 2. Suffix change — a perfective "core" verb, with an imperfective built off it

Some pairs work the other direction: the perfective is the simpler-looking stem, and the imperfective adds a suffix like **-ива-/-ыва-** or **-ва-** to turn a single completed action into a repeatable/ongoing process.

**купи́ть** (perfective, "to buy — one purchase") is the base; **покупа́ть** (imperfective, "to buy — as an activity/repeatedly") is built by changing the stem and adding -а- in a pattern that (once you've seen a few) becomes recognizable: купи́ть → покупа́ть.

**дать** (perfective "to give — one act of giving") vs. **дава́ть** (imperfective, "to give — as a repeated or ongoing activity") shows the -ва- imperfectivizing suffix directly.

**Я покупа́ю проду́кты ка́ждую неде́лю.** — lit. *"I buy(imperf) groceries every week."* — "I buy groceries every week." (habitual)

**Я купи́л проду́кты вчера́.** — lit. *"I through-bought(perf) groceries yesterday."* — "I bought the groceries yesterday." (one completed event)

### 3. Different roots entirely (suppletion)

A few very common pairs simply use two unrelated words — you have to memorize these as a pair with no shared shape at all.

**говори́ть / сказа́ть** — говори́ть (imperfective, "to speak/talk/say" as an activity) and сказа́ть (perfective, "to say" one specific thing, completed) look nothing alike.

**брать / взять** — брать (imperfective, "to take") and взять (perfective, "to take" — one grab, completed) are also unrelated roots.

**Он говори́л о рабо́те час.** — lit. *"He spoke about work-PREP hour."* — "He talked about work for an hour." (process, duration)

**Он сказа́л одно́ сло́во.** — lit. *"He said one word-ACC."* — "He said one word." (single completed utterance)

## Aspect in the past tense

This is where aspect choice becomes unavoidable — you cannot form a Russian past-tense sentence without picking imperfective or perfective, and the choice changes the meaning.

**Я чита́л кни́гу.** — lit. *"I was-reading book-ACC."* — "I was reading a book / I read a book (at some point — no claim it's finished)."

**Я прочита́л кни́гу.** — lit. *"I through-read book-ACC."* — "I read the whole book (and finished it)."

The imperfective sentence leaves the outcome open — maybe you finished, maybe you got bored on page 40 and stopped, maybe you're describing what you were doing when the phone rang. The perfective sentence makes a specific claim: the book is done.

More contrast pairs:

**Я писа́л письмо́.** — lit. *"I was-writing letter-ACC."* — "I was writing a letter." (in progress, or just "I did some letter-writing" — unclear if finished)

**Я написа́л письмо́.** — lit. *"I onto-wrote letter-ACC."* — "I wrote the letter." (it's done, ready to send)

**Мы обе́дали, когда́ ты позвони́л.** — lit. *"We were-dining, when you through-called."* — "We were having lunch when you called." (ongoing background action + single interrupting event — this is a classic imperfective/perfective pairing in one sentence)

**Вчера́ ве́чером я гото́вил у́жин.** — lit. *"Yesterday evening I was-preparing dinner-ACC."* — "Last night I was making dinner." (describes the scene, no claim about whether dinner got finished)

**Вчера́ ве́чером я пригото́вил у́жин.** — lit. *"Yesterday evening I through-prepared dinner-ACC."* — "Last night I made dinner." (a completed event — dinner exists now)

## Aspect in the future

You met the two future systems in [Lesson 8](08-verbs-past-future.md); aspect is exactly what decides which one you use.

**Я бу́ду чита́ть кни́гу за́втра.** — lit. *"I will-be to-read book-ACC tomorrow."* — "I'll be reading the book tomorrow." (a planned activity/process, no claim of finishing it)

**Я прочита́ю кни́гу за́втра.** — lit. *"I through-read(perfective-future) book-ACC tomorrow."* — "I'll (finish) read(ing) the book tomorrow." (a completed event by the end of tomorrow)

**Мы бу́дем гото́вить у́жин.** — lit. *"We will-be to-prepare dinner-ACC."* — "We'll be making dinner." (the activity itself, could take a while, no promise it's done by any point)

**Мы пригото́вим у́жин к семи́.** — lit. *"We through-prepare(perf-future) dinner-ACC by seven."* — "We'll have dinner ready by seven." (a specific, completed result by a deadline)

## Aspect with negation

This is a subtle but genuinely important nuance that trips up even advanced learners.

**Negated imperfective** = a neutral statement that something didn't happen — no implication of failure or missed expectation.

**Я не звони́л ей.** — lit. *"I not was-calling her."* — "I didn't call her." (plain fact — maybe I never intended to, no big deal)

**Negated perfective** = there was a specific, expected, or planned action, and it *failed to happen* — a missed event, often with a sense of consequence or "should have."

**Я не позвони́л ей.** — lit. *"I not through-called her."* — "I didn't call her (like I was supposed to / and now there's a problem)."

Compare these two side by side: both are grammatically fine "I didn't call her" sentences, but the second one carries the flavor of "...and that was a mistake" or "...and I meant to." Context usually makes clear which one fits — if you're describing something you were expected or planning to do, lean perfective; if you're just stating an absence of action, lean imperfective.

**Он не де́лал дома́шнее зада́ние вчера́.** — lit. *"He not was-doing homework-ACC yesterday."* — "He wasn't doing homework yesterday." (neutral — describes what he was up to, or wasn't)

**Он не сде́лал дома́шнее зада́ние.** — lit. *"He not through-did homework-ACC."* — "He didn't get his homework done." (implies it was due, expected, and now it's a problem)

## Aspect in infinitives: which verbs demand which aspect

Some verbs that take an infinitive don't let you choose freely — they lock in one aspect.

### хоте́ть (to want) — either aspect, meaning shifts

**Я хочу́ чита́ть.** — lit. *"I want to-read(imperf)."* — "I want to read (as an activity — I'm in the mood to read)."

**Я хочу́ прочита́ть э́ту кни́гу.** — lit. *"I want to-through-read(perf) this book-ACC."* — "I want to finish/get through this book." (a specific completed result in mind)

### нача́ть / продолжа́ть / зако́нчить — imperfective infinitive **only**

Verbs meaning "start," "continue," and "finish" describe a *phase* of an action — and a phase only makes sense applied to something viewed as an ongoing process, not something already conceived as a single, indivisible completed point. So these verbs always take an **imperfective** infinitive, never a perfective one.

**Я на́чал чита́ть кни́гу.** — lit. *"I began to-read(imperf) book-ACC."* — "I started reading the book."

*Not* "на́чал прочита́ть" — you can't "start" something that's defined as already-finished; "прочита́ть" packages the whole action as one completed unit, and a completed unit has no "start point" to begin separately.

**Он продолжа́л рабо́тать.** — lit. *"He continued to-work(imperf)."* — "He kept working."

**Мы зако́нчили гото́вить у́жин.** — lit. *"We finished to-prepare(imperf) dinner-ACC."* — "We finished making dinner."

This rule (нача́ть / продолжа́ть / зако́нчить + imperfective infinitive, always) is worth memorizing as a fixed chunk — it's one of the few completely reliable rules in the whole aspect system.

## Decision steps: how to choose aspect in practice

When you're building a sentence and aren't sure which aspect to reach for, walk through these questions in order:

1. **Is this a repeated, habitual, or ongoing/in-progress action, with no focus on a single completed result?** → Use **imperfective**. ("I read every evening," "I was cooking when you arrived," "She speaks Russian.")
2. **Is this describing one single action, and are you focused on its completion or result?** → Use **perfective**. ("I finished the book," "She wrote the letter and sent it," "We'll have dinner ready by seven.")
3. **Is this negated?** Ask: are you just stating that something didn't happen (no implication of failure) or that a specific expected/planned action was missed? → Neutral absence = **imperfective**; missed/failed specific event = **perfective**.
4. **Is the verb one of нача́ть / продолжа́ть / зако́нчить (or similar phase verbs) taking an infinitive?** → The infinitive must be **imperfective**, regardless of the overall meaning of the sentence.
5. **Still unsure?** Default to imperfective for general statements of fact or ability ("Я чита́ю по-ру́сски" — "I read Russian," i.e., I have the ability) and reach for perfective only when you can point to one specific, complete event you have in mind.

## More contrast pairs to internalize

**Он гото́вил обе́д, когда́ я пришёл.** — lit. *"He was-preparing lunch, when I arrived."* — "He was making lunch when I arrived." (imperfective background action)

**Он пригото́вил обе́д до того́, как я пришёл.** — lit. *"He through-prepared lunch before that, as I arrived."* — "He had lunch ready before I arrived." (perfective, completed result)

**Я учи́л ру́сский язы́к два го́да.** — lit. *"I was-teaching-self Russian language two years."* — "I studied/was studying Russian for two years." (process over a stretch of time, no claim of mastery)

**Я вы́учил э́то стихотворе́ние.** — lit. *"I out-learned this poem-ACC."* — "I learned/memorized this poem." (a completed result — you now know it by heart)

**Ты чита́л э́ту кни́гу?** — lit. *"You read(imperf) this book-ACC?"* — "Have you (ever) read this book?" (asking about the fact/experience — did this ever happen)

**Ты прочита́л э́ту кни́гу?** — lit. *"You through-read(perf) this book-ACC?"* — "Did you finish this book?" (asking about a specific, expected completion — e.g. the one you started last week)

**Мы бу́дем смотре́ть фильм.** — lit. *"We will-be to-watch(imperf) film-ACC."* — "We're going to watch a movie." (the activity of watching, however long it takes)

**Мы посмо́трим фильм за́втра.** — lit. *"We through-look-at(perf-future) film-ACC tomorrow."* — "We'll watch the movie tomorrow." (viewed as one bounded event that will happen and conclude)

**Я не пил ко́фе сего́дня.** — lit. *"I not was-drinking coffee today."* — "I haven't had coffee today." (neutral statement of fact)

**Я не вы́пил ко́фе, кото́рый ты дал мне.** — lit. *"I not out-drank coffee-ACC, which you gave me."* — "I didn't (get to) finish the coffee you gave me." (a specific cup, a specific missed completion)

## A note on verbs without a "normal" pair

Not every verb has a clean imperfective/perfective partner — a few verbs (жить, "to live"; рабо́тать, "to work," in its basic sense) are imperfective-only, because "living" or general "working" don't naturally package into single completed events. Don't panic if a pair doesn't leap out from the dictionary — at A2, focus on internalizing the ~30–40 highest-frequency pairs (starting with the table at the top of this lesson) rather than trying to find a perfective partner for everything.
