# Gender & Nouns

!!! abstract "In a nutshell"

    - Every Russian noun has a grammatical gender: masculine, feminine, or neuter. You can usually tell from the last letter.
    - Nouns ending in **ь** are ambiguous — could be masculine or feminine, and you have to memorize which.
    - A few nouns are grammatically feminine-looking but refer to people who are naturally male (папа, дедушка) — natural gender wins.
    - Plurals mostly follow -ы/-и (and -а/-я for neuter), but there's a spelling rule and a short list of common irregulars you need to just know.

Gender is the foundation everything else sits on. Every case ending you'll learn in the next five lessons depends on a noun's gender — so getting this automatic now saves you from re-learning it five times over.

## How to tell gender from the ending

Look at the last letter of the dictionary (nominative singular) form.

| Ending | Gender | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| consonant | masculine | стол (table), дом (house), студе́нт (student) |
| **-а / -я** | feminine | кни́га (book), маши́на (car), неде́ля (week) |
| **-о / -е** | neuter | окно́ (window), мо́ре (sea) |
| **-ь** | ambiguous — masc. or fem. | see below |

For roughly 90% of nouns, this table is all you need: no ending → masculine, -а/-я → feminine, -о/-е → neuter.

## The -ь problem

Nouns ending in a soft sign, **ь**, can be either masculine or feminine — the spelling doesn't tell you. You have to learn the gender with the word, the same way you'd learn the gender of a French or German noun.

| Word | Gender | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| день | masculine | day |
| слова́рь | masculine | dictionary |
| учи́тель | masculine | teacher (male) |
| дверь | feminine | door |
| ночь | feminine | night |
| крова́ть | feminine | bed |

A rough (not perfect) tendency: -ь words for professions/roles and abstract "action" nouns skew masculine, while body-part and everyday-object -ь words skew feminine — but this is a tendency, not a rule. When you learn a new -ь noun, learn its gender at the same time, the way you'd note "der/die/das" for a German word.

## Natural gender overrides spelling

A handful of nouns end in **-а/-я** (which normally signals feminine) but refer to male people. For these, **natural gender wins** — they behave grammatically as masculine.

| Word | Meaning | Behaves as |
|---|---|---|
| па́па | dad | masculine |
| де́душка | grandpa | masculine |
| дя́дя | uncle | masculine |
| мужчи́на | man | masculine |

**Мой па́па до́ма.** — lit. *"My-MASC dad-NOM home."* — "My dad is home."

Notice «мой» (my, masculine) rather than «моя́» (my, feminine) — because «па́па» refers to a man, everything that agrees with it uses masculine forms, even though the noun itself ends in -а like a feminine noun.

## Plurals

### The basic pattern

| Singular ending | Plural ending | Example |
|---|---|---|
| hard consonant (masc.) | **-ы** | стол → столы́ |
| soft consonant / -ь (masc.) | **-и** | слова́рь → словари́ |
| -а (fem.) | **-ы** | кни́га → кни́ги* |
| -я (fem.) | **-и** | неде́ля → неде́ли |
| -ь (fem.) | **-и** | дверь → две́ри |
| -о (neut.) | **-а** | окно́ → о́кна |
| -е (neut.) | **-я** | мо́ре → моря́ |

*«кни́га» → «кни́ги» looks like it breaks the -а→-ы pattern — that's the spelling rule below in action, not an exception to gender logic.

### The 7-letter spelling rule

Russian spelling forbids writing **ы** after seven specific letters. If a plural ending would normally be **-ы**, but the noun's last consonant is one of these seven, you write **-и** instead:

> **After г, к, х, ж, ш, щ, ч — always write и, never ы.**

| Word | Last consonant | Expected | Actually written |
|---|---|---|---|
| кни́га | г | книг**ы** | **кни́ги** |
| студе́нтка | к | студенток**ы** | **студе́нтки** |
| ру́чка | к | ручк**ы** | **ру́чки** |
| нога́ | г | ног**ы** | **но́ги** |

This same rule reaches beyond plurals — it's *why* «жить» and «маши́на» are spelled with и rather than ы even though ж and ш are hard consonants (see [Alphabet & Pronunciation §5](../alphabet.md)). One spelling rule, two places you'll see it. Memorize the seven letters as a group: **г к х ж ш щ ч**.

### Common irregular plurals

These don't follow any ending pattern — just learn them as vocabulary, the way you learned "child → children" in English.

| Singular | Plural | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| челове́к | лю́ди | person → people |
| ребёнок | де́ти | child → children |
| друг | друзья́ | friend → friends |
| брат | бра́тья | brother → brothers |
| сын | сыновья́ | son → sons |
| стул | сту́лья | chair → chairs |
| мать | ма́тери | mother → mothers |
| дочь | до́чери | daughter → daughters |

**У меня́ есть друзья́.** — lit. *"At me exist friends-NOM."* — "I have friends."

Note «друзья́» — not «дру́ги» — for the plural of «друг». This is exactly the kind of irregular you just have to drill until it's automatic.

## Putting it together

**Э́то большо́й стол.** — lit. *"This big-MASC table-NOM."* — "This is a big table." (masc., no ending)

**Э́то но́вая кни́га.** — lit. *"This new-FEM book-NOM."* — "This is a new book." (fem., -а)

**Э́то ста́рое окно́.** — lit. *"This old-NEUT window-NOM."* — "This is an old window." (neut., -о)

The adjective endings agreeing with each gender (большо́й / но́вая / ста́рое) are covered in full in [Adjectives & Possessives](10-adjectives-possessives.md) — for now just notice that gender ripples outward from the noun to everything describing it. That's why nailing gender now pays off in every lesson from here on, starting with [Accusative](02-accusative.md).

## Practice

Try each one before checking the answer.

1. Give the gender of: стол, ко́мната, окно́, слова́рь, ночь, па́па.
2. Give the gender of: неде́ля, дом, мо́ре, дверь, дя́дя, кни́га.
3. Form the plural of: студе́нт, кни́га, окно́, ру́чка.
4. Form the plural of: мо́ре, стол, неде́ля, нога́.
5. Give the irregular plural of: ребёнок, челове́к, друг, брат.
6. Why is «кни́ги» spelled with и rather than ы?
7. Explain why «мой де́душка» uses «мой» (masculine) even though «де́душка» ends in -а.
8. Translate: "This is my friend (m.)." (use «мой друг»)
9. Translate: "I have children." (use «у меня́ есть»)
10. Sort these into masc./fem./neut.: тетра́дь, письмо́, музе́й, семья́, у́тро.

??? success "Answers"

    1. стол — masc. (consonant ending); ко́мната — fem. (-а); окно́ — neut. (-о); слова́рь — masc. (-ь, learned as masculine); ночь — fem. (-ь, learned as feminine); па́па — masc. (natural gender overrides the -а ending).
    2. неде́ля — fem. (-я); дом — masc. (consonant); мо́ре — neut. (-е); дверь — fem. (-ь, learned as feminine); дя́дя — masc. (natural gender); кни́га — fem. (-а).
    3. студе́нт → студе́нты (masc. consonant → -ы); кни́га → кни́ги (spelling rule: г blocks ы, use и); окно́ → о́кна (neut. -о → -а); ру́чка → ру́чки (spelling rule: к blocks ы, use и).
    4. мо́ре → моря́ (neut. -е → -я); стол → столы́ (masc. consonant → -ы, no spelling-rule letter involved); неде́ля → неде́ли (fem. -я → -и); нога́ → но́ги (spelling rule: г blocks ы, use и).
    5. ребёнок → де́ти; челове́к → лю́ди; друг → друзья́; брат → бра́тья. All four are memorized irregulars, not pattern-based.
    6. Because г is one of the seven letters (г к х ж ш щ ч) after which ы can never be written — и is substituted automatically, regardless of gender logic.
    7. «Де́душка» ends in -а (grammatically feminine-looking) but refers to a man — natural gender overrides the spelling, so everything agreeing with it (мой, not моя́) takes masculine form.
    8. **Э́то мой друг.** — lit. *"This my-MASC friend-NOM."* — "This is my friend."
    9. **У меня́ есть де́ти.** — lit. *"At me exist children-NOM."* — "I have children." (де́ти is the irregular plural of ребёнок)
    10. тетра́дь — fem. (-ь, learned as feminine, "notebook"); письмо́ — neut. (-о, "letter"); музе́й — masc. (ends in the consonant-like й, "museum"); семья́ — fem. (-я, "family"); у́тро — neut. (-о, "morning").
