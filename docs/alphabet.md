# Alphabet & Pronunciation

!!! abstract "In a nutshell"

    - Stress is unpredictable and unmarked in normal text — you have to learn it per word. It also **moves the sound** of vowels around it.
    - Unstressed **о → "a"**, unstressed **е/я → "i"**. This is not sloppy speech; it's the standard pronunciation.
    - Consonants come in hard/soft pairs. Softness is shown by the *following letter* (ь or a soft vowel), not by the consonant itself.
    - Ж, Ш, Ц are always hard. Ч, Щ are always soft — no exceptions, even when spelled with a vowel that "should" soften or harden them.
    - Voiced consonants devoice at the end of a word, and consonant clusters assimilate in voicing. Some letters in clusters are just silent.

You already know the letter shapes. This page is about turning "sounding it out" into fluent reading — the rules that make Russian words sound like Russian instead of a string of individually-correct sounds.

## 1. Stress is everything

Every Russian word of more than one syllable has exactly one stressed syllable, and it is **not marked** in normal writing (dictionaries and textbooks add a mark, ́, over the vowel — this site does that too, in vocab and ending tables). You simply have to learn where the stress falls when you learn the word.

Stress matters for two reasons:

1. Some words are distinguished *only* by stress: **за́мок** ("castle") vs **замо́к** ("lock").
2. Stress determines how unstressed vowels are pronounced — the next two sections.

## 2. Vowel reduction

Unstressed vowels in Russian don't get their "textbook" sound. They get shortened and pulled toward more neutral sounds. This happens in every single word with more than one syllable, so it's not optional — it's what fluent Russian sounds like.

### о → "a"

An unstressed **о** is pronounced like a short **"a"** (like the "a" in English "sofa").

| Word | Written | Pronounced (rough) | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| молоко́ | mo-lo-KO | ma-la-KO | stress is on the last o; the first two are unstressed |
| хорошо́ | kho-ro-SHO | kha-ra-SHO | same pattern |
| Москва́ | MOSK-va | MASK-va | stress on the second syllable |

This is why Russians learning English often need to be told "the O is a real O" — in their own language, an unstressed о almost never is.

### е / я → "i"

An unstressed **е** or **я** is pronounced like a short **"i"** (like "i" in "bit").

| Word | Written | Pronounced (rough) | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| теле́фон | te-le-FON | ti-li-FON | both е's before the stress reduce toward "i" |
| язы́к | ya-ZYK | yi-ZYK | unstressed я → "yi" |
| меня́ | me-NYA | mi-NYA | unstressed е → "i"; the я is stressed, stays "ya" |

**The pattern to internalize:** stressed vowels keep their full, clear sound. Every unstressed vowel gets compressed toward a shorter, more central sound — о and а both drift toward "a", е and я both drift toward "i". This is *why* native speakers seem to swallow syllables: they're not being lazy, they're following the rule.

## 3. Final-consonant devoicing

Russian consonants come in voiced/voiceless pairs:

| Voiced | б | в | г | д | ж | з |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Voiceless | п | ф | к | т | ш | с |

**A voiced consonant at the very end of a word is pronounced as its voiceless partner.**

| Word | Meaning | Looks like | Sounds like |
|---|---|---|---|
| год | year | "god" | **"got"** |
| хлеб | bread | "khleb" | **"khlep"** |
| друг | friend | "drug" | **"druk"** |
| глаз | eye | "glaz" | **"glas"** |
| нож | knife | "nozh" | **"nosh"** |

This is why «Смирно́в» (a surname ending in a voiced в) sounds like it ends in "-off" — and why so many Russian surnames get spelled "-off" in English transliteration.

## 4. Voicing assimilation in clusters

When two consonants sit next to each other, the *second* one determines whether the whole cluster is voiced or voiceless — the first consonant assimilates to match it.

| Word | Cluster | What happens |
|---|---|---|
| во́дка | дк | д (voiced) becomes т before the voiceless к → sounds like "**вотка**" |
| сва́дьба | дьб | д becomes voiced-matching before б — stays voiced (б is voiced) |
| ло́жка | жк | ж (voiced) becomes ш before voiceless к → "**лошка**" |
| футбо́л | тб | т (voiceless) becomes д before voiced б → "**фудбол**" |

You don't need to memorize every cluster rule right now — just know that **consonants next to each other influence each other**, so a word's pronunciation can differ from a strict letter-by-letter reading.

## 5. Hard and soft consonants

This is the single most important structural idea in Russian pronunciation, and Duolingo never explains it.

Almost every Russian consonant has two versions: **hard** (regular) and **soft** (palatalized — pronounce it with the middle of your tongue raised toward the roof of your mouth, roughly like adding a fast "y" glide right after the consonant).

Russian doesn't spell hard and soft consonants with different letters. Instead, **the letter that follows the consonant tells you which version to use**:

| After a consonant... | Consonant is | Example |
|---|---|---|
| а, о, у, ы, э | **hard** | **т**а, **т**о, **т**у |
| я, ё, ю, и, е | **soft** | **т**я, **т**ё, **т**ю, **т**и, **т**е |
| ь (soft sign) | **soft** | ма**т**ь |
| ъ (hard sign) | **hard** (blocks softening across a prefix boundary) | подъе́зд |

So я, ё, ю, е, и aren't just "vowels" — they're instructions: *"soften the consonant before me."* Compare:

**мат** — pronounced with a hard т (like English "t")
**мать** — pronounced with a soft т (tongue pressed toward the roof of the mouth) — means "mother"

This is a real, meaning-changing distinction, not a subtle accent detail: **брат** (brother, hard т) vs a soft-т word sound genuinely different to a Russian ear, the same way "sh" and "s" sound different to you.

**Practical rule of thumb:** when you see я/ё/ю/е/и right after a consonant, don't pronounce them as a separate "ya/yo/yu/ye/yi" sound glued on — they mostly just signal a soft consonant, then contribute their vowel color (я→a, ё→o, ю→u, е→e, и→i quality) inside that softness.

### Always-hard: ж ш ц

These three consonants are **always hard**, no matter what vowel follows. If you see и after ж or ш, it's still pronounced like ы (a hard, back "i"):

- **жи**ть ("to live") — the и does *not* soften ж; sounds close to "жы"
- **ши**на ("tire") — same deal, sounds close to "шы"
- **ц**ирк ("circus") — ц stays hard even before и

This is also why the spelling rule "**жи, ши** — always written with и, never ы" exists (more on this in the [Gender & Nouns](grammar/01-gender-and-nouns.md) lesson) — it's a pure spelling convention that doesn't change the hard pronunciation.

### Always-soft: ч щ

These two are **always soft**, no matter what vowel follows:

- **ча**с ("hour") — sounds like "cha" with an inherently soft ч, even though a follows (which would normally signal "hard")
- **чу**вство ("feeling") — same, ч stays soft before у
- **щ** is always soft and is often pronounced like a long, soft "shsh"

**Takeaway:** ж/ш/ц and ч/щ are exceptions to the "next letter decides softness" rule — for these six letters, hardness/softness is fixed and the following vowel just contributes its own sound.

## 6. Silent letters in clusters

Some consonant clusters in Russian have a letter that simply isn't pronounced — a spelling fossil, similar to the silent "t" in English "listen."

| Word | Meaning | Silent letter | Sounds like |
|---|---|---|---|
| здра́вствуйте | hello (formal) | the first **в** drops | **"здра́ствуйте"** |
| со́лнце | sun | the **л** drops | **"со́нце"** |
| по́здно | late | the **д** drops | **"по́зно"** |
| се́рдце | heart | the **д** drops | **"се́рце"** |
| чу́вство | feeling | the **в** drops | **"чу́ство"** |

There's no single rule that predicts these — they're a short, learnable list. The five above cover the highest-frequency cases you'll run into at A2.

    Sounds like **MAT'** with a soft т (tongue toward the roof of the mouth) — different from **мат** (hard т, a vulgar word). The ь signals softness.
