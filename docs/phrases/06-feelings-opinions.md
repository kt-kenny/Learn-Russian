# Feelings & Opinions

Liking things, agreeing/disagreeing, and saying how you feel. This page is where the [dative](../grammar/05-dative.md) case really earns its keep — Russian routes almost all feelings and opinions through "to me," not "I."

## Liking and disliking

| Russian | Literal | Means | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Мне нра́вится...** | "To-me is-pleasing..." | "I like..." | Dative мне + verb agreeing with the *thing* liked, not with "I" — see [dative](../grammar/05-dative.md). |
| **Мне нра́вятся...** | "To-me are-pleasing..." | "I like..." (plural things) | нра́вятся (plural) when the liked thing is plural: Мне нра́вятся соба́ки. |
| **Мне не нра́вится...** | "To-me not is-pleasing..." | "I don't like..." | не goes right before the verb, not before мне. |
| **Тебе́ нра́вится?** | "To-you is-pleasing?" (informal) | "Do you like it?" | Informal — swap тебе́ for вам to make it formal. |
| **Мне о́чень нра́вится** | "To-me very is-pleasing" | "I really like it" | о́чень intensifies, sits before нра́вится. |
| **Я люблю́...** | "I love..." | "I love... / I'm into..." | Unlike нра́вится, люблю́ conjugates normally with "I" as subject — used for deeper/habitual liking: Я люблю́ ко́фе ("I love coffee" as a general truth). |

!!! tip "Pattern"

    Мне нра́вится/нра́вятся is a dative-subject construction: the person doing the liking (мне) is grammatically the indirect object, and the thing liked (nominative) controls the verb ending. This flips English logic — "I like dogs" becomes, word for word, "To me are-pleasing dogs." See [dative](../grammar/05-dative.md) for the full case table.

!!! tip "Pattern"

    Нра́вится vs. люблю́ is a real distinction, not free variation: нра́вится leans toward "this specific thing appeals to me" (a dish, a movie, a person you just met), while люблю́ leans toward deep or habitual love (Я люблю́ Росси́ю, "I love Russia"). When unsure, нра́вится is the safer, more common default.

## Opinions and agreement

| Russian | Literal | Means | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Я ду́маю, что...** | "I think, that..." | "I think that..." | что here is the conjunction "that," required — Russian doesn't drop it the way English can. |
| **По-мо́ему...** | "By-my [opinion]..." | "In my opinion..." | Fixed adverbial form — doesn't decline further. |
| **Я согла́сен / согла́сна** | "I [am] agreed (m/f)" | "I agree" | Short-form adjective; согла́сен for men, согла́сна for women. |
| **Я не согла́сен / не согла́сна** | "I [am] not agreed (m/f)" | "I disagree" | Same short-form adjective, negated. |
| **Коне́чно** | "Of-course" | "Of course" | Note pronunciation: the ч is pronounced "sh" here — "коне́шно." |
| **Мо́жет быть** | "May to-be" | "Maybe" | Fixed phrase — literally "it may be." |
| **Наве́рное** | "Probably" | "Probably" | Common filler/hedge word in everyday speech. |
| **То́чно!** | "Exactly!" / "Precisely!" | "Exactly!" | Also used to mean "for sure" agreeing with a plan. |
| **Ты прав / ты права́** | "You [are] right (m/f)" | "You're right" | Short-form adjective agreeing with the *listener's* gender, not the speaker's. |
| **Возмо́жно** | "Possibly" | "Possibly" / "It's possible" | Slightly more formal than мо́жет быть. |

!!! tip "Pattern"

    Short-form adjectives (согла́сен/согла́сна, прав/права́, сыт/сыта́ from the food page) always describe a temporary state of the subject and always agree in gender/number: -0 for masculine, -а for feminine, -о for neuter, -ы for plural. This is a distinct pattern from the long-form adjectives you use to describe nouns directly — see [adjectives](../grammar/10-adjectives-possessives.md).

## Reacting: interesting, boring, and more

| Russian | Literal | Means | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Как интере́сно!** | "How interesting!" | "That's interesting!" | Exclamatory adverb — no verb needed. |
| **Как ску́чно** | "How boring" | "How boring" | Same construction, opposite meaning. |
| **Э́то интере́сно** | "This [is] interesting" | "That's interesting" | Predicative neuter adverb form, not a long adjective — matches the "no to-be verb" pattern. |
| **Э́то ску́чно** | "This [is] boring" | "That's boring" | Same predicative pattern. |
| **Здо́рово!** | "Great!" | "Awesome!" / "Cool!" | Casual enthusiasm — note stress on the second syllable, unlike здоро́вье ("health"). |
| **Как жаль** | "How [a] pity" | "What a shame" | жаль is an invariant predicate word, doesn't conjugate. |
| **Не мо́жет быть!** | "Not may to-be!" | "No way!" / "That can't be!" | Common surprised reaction. |
| **Ужа́сно** | "Terribly" | "Terrible / awful" | Predicative adverb, same family as интере́сно/ску́чно. |

!!! tip "Pattern"

    Neuter short adjectives used with no subject (интере́сно, ску́чно, ужа́сно, жаль) are Russia's all-purpose reaction words — they don't describe a specific noun, they describe the situation. This is the same grammatical slot as impersonal feeling words below (ску́чно, ве́село), just applied to opinions instead of your own state.

## How you feel: impersonal dative constructions

| Russian | Literal | Means | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Мне ску́чно** | "To-me [it is] boringly" | "I'm bored" | Impersonal dative — the feeling happens *to* you, you don't "do" boredom. See [dative](../grammar/05-dative.md). |
| **Мне ве́село** | "To-me [it is] funly" | "I'm having fun" | Same impersonal pattern, positive version. |
| **Мне хо́лодно** | "To-me [it is] coldly" | "I'm cold" | Physical sensations use this pattern too, not just emotions. |
| **Мне жа́рко** | "To-me [it is] hotly" | "I'm hot" | Opposite of мне хо́лодно. |
| **Мне гру́стно** | "To-me [it is] sadly" | "I'm sad" | Same family as мне ску́чно. |
| **Я уста́л / уста́ла** | "I [am] tired (m/f)" | "I'm tired" | Short-form *past participle*, not impersonal dative — agrees with the subject like согла́сен does. |
| **Я сча́стлив / сча́стлива** | "I [am] happy (m/f)" | "I'm happy" | Short-form adjective, subject-agreeing — contrast with the мне + adverb group above. |
| **Я го́лоден / голодна́** | "I [am] hungry (m/f)" | "I'm hungry" | Short-form adjective; note the stress shift and fleeting о in the masculine form (го́лоден, not "голоден" evenly stressed). |
| **Мне ка́жется, что...** | "To-me it-seems, that..." | "It seems to me that..." | ка́жется is dative-governing, reflexive, and takes что like ду́маю does. |

!!! tip "Pattern"

    Two competing patterns look similar but aren't: **Мне + neuter adverb** (мне ску́чно, мне хо́лодно, мне ве́село) describes something happening *to* you, no subject involved — closer to "it is boring to me." **Я + short adjective** (я уста́л, я го́лоден, я сча́стлив) describes a state *of* you, and the ending agrees with your own gender. Both translate to "I'm X" in English, but they're grammatically opposite constructions — that's why мне нра́вится and мне ску́чно behave alike, while я уста́л and я согла́сен behave alike.
