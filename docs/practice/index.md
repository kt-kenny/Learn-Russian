# Practice — Overview

Grammar you've only read is grammar you don't actually have. These drills are where a rule turns into a reflex. Don't skip them, and don't do them in your head — the whole point is catching your own mistakes on paper, where you can see them.

## How to drill

1. **Write your answers by hand** (or type them, but commit to an answer) before looking at anything else. Guessing and being wrong is part of the process — it's how you find out what you actually don't know yet, as opposed to what you *recognize* when you see it.
2. **Check against the answer key.** Every drill's answers are hidden in a collapsible block — click to expand only after you've written every item in the set. Checking one at a time defeats the purpose; you want to see your own pattern of mistakes across the whole set.
3. **For every wrong answer, write down *why* you missed it.** Not just "wrong" — the actual rule. "Forgot animate accusative = genitive for masculine nouns" is useful. A checkmark or an X is not. The answer keys give you the rule for every item so you can compare your reasoning to the real one.
4. **Redo only the ones you got wrong**, a day or two later. If you missed it because you didn't know the rule, go re-read the grammar page first — drilling a rule you don't know just cements the wrong guess.

## When to drill relative to grammar lessons

Drill *after* reading a grammar lesson, not instead of it, and not before. The order that works:

1. Read the grammar lesson (twice — once fast, once slow with the tables).
2. Do the matching drill section within a day or two, while the lesson is fresh.
3. Come back to a mixed/cumulative drill (like [Case Drills §4](case-drills.md) or [Aspect Choice](verb-drills.md)) a few weeks later, once several lessons have stacked up — this is what actually tests retention instead of short-term recall.

The [roadmap](../roadmap.md) already schedules this for you week by week. If you're following it, you'll hit each drill section right when it's useful.

## A note on the answer keys

Russian often allows more than one correct way to say something — word order is flexible, and there are frequently two acceptable synonyms. Where a drill has a genuinely acceptable alternative answer, it's listed. If your answer differs from the model answer in a way that isn't listed and you're not sure whether it's still correct, that's a good question to bring to a tutor or a fluent speaker rather than guess — see [Resources](../resources.md) for where to find one.
